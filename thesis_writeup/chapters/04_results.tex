\section{10-Bar Planar Truss}
\label{sec:results:10bar}

The 10-bar planar truss of Sunar and Belegundu~\cite{sunar1991} is the
most widely reported sizing benchmark in the literature and is used
here as the primary validation case for the classical optimisation
layer. Geometry, loads, material properties, and stress and
displacement limits are reproduced without modification from the
original formulation and are tabulated in
\cref{tab:results:10bar-setup}.

Three algorithms were run against this benchmark with the default

to both the original stress and displacement limits and the
IS~800:2007 checks described in \cref{sec:method:is800}.

Particle-swarm optimisation most closely reproduces the published
optimum, with a best weight of $5061.05$~lb corresponding to a
relative error of $0.004\%$ --- effectively machine-level agreement
with Sunar and Belegundu's reported value. The genetic algorithm
converges to within $0.04\%$ across ten independent seeds with a
sample standard deviation of $7.11$~lb, which is consistent with the
$\pm 2\%$ tolerance specified in the Phase~1 validation gate. NSGA-II,
used here in single-objective-weight mode as a smoke-test of the
multi-objective wrapper, returns a slightly heavier best design at
$5081.51$~lb (percent error $0.41\%$) and is primarily exercised later
in \cref{sec:results:pareto} for its multi-objective capabilities.

\section{25-Bar Spatial Truss}
\label{sec:results:25bar}

The 25-bar spatial transmission tower of Schmit and Miura~%
\cite{schmit1976} is the canonical three-dimensional sizing benchmark.
Our encoding follows the standard 10-node / 25-bar geometry with 8
symmetry groups and uniform $\pm 40{,}000$~psi stress limits — a
common modern simplification used in Hasançebi~\cite{kaveh2010} and
Kaveh~\cite{kaveh2010}. Problem parameters are summarised in
\cref{tab:results:25bar-setup}.

Across all three optimisers the displacement constraint is active at
its $0.35$~in bound. With $\mathrm{pop}=100$ and $n_\text{gen}=500$,

minimum: best-of-seeds weights of \num{545.30}~lb (GA),

uniform $\pm 40$~ksi stress limits rather than Schmit's member-type-

tightest cross-seed spread ($\sigma = \num{0.09}$~lb), consistent with
its behaviour on the 10-bar benchmark, while GA shows the widest
spread ($\sigma = \num{1.11}$~lb). All runs are feasible against both
the native stress/displacement constraints and the IS~800:2007
compliance check described in \cref{sec:method:is800}.

\section{72-Bar Spatial Tower}
\label{sec:results:72bar}

The 72-bar spatial tower of Fleury and Schmit~\cite{fleury1980},
popularised in Erbatur et al.~\cite{erbatur2000} and Camp \&
Bichon~\cite{camp2004}, is a four-storey cantilever tower with $120
\times 120$~in square plan and 240~in total height. 20 nodes, 72 bars,
16 symmetry groups arranged per storey as legs (4), face diagonals
(8), top horizontals (4), plan diagonals (2) — the canonical Camp
ordering, so published designs from the literature can be plugged in
directly. The canonical benchmark carries two load cases: an
asymmetric tip load (LC1) and a uniform vertical compression at all
four tip nodes (LC2). The displacement constraint applies only to the
lateral ($x$ and $y$) components at the four tip nodes (12--15);
vertical and lower-storey displacements are unconstrained. Parameters
are listed in \cref{tab:results:72bar-setup}.

    \midrule
    Nodes                   & 20 (3D)                & --- \\
    Elements                & 72                     & --- \\
    Design variables (groups)& 16                    & --- \\
    Young's modulus $E$     & $1.0 \times 10^{7}$    & psi \\
    Density $\rho$          & $0.1$                  & lb/in\textsuperscript{3} \\
    Load cases              & 2 (LC1 + LC2)          & --- \\
    Stress limit            & $\pm 25{,}000$         & psi \\

All three algorithms converge to the same neighbourhood of $\sim
\num{550}$~lb with cross-seed spreads under $\num{1}\,\%$, well above
the literature-cited minimum of $\num{379.62}$~lb. This gap warrants
explanation. We adopt the canonical Camp \& Bichon~\cite{camp2004}
problem definition exactly: identical group order, lateral-tip-only
displacement check, and the two standard load cases. Yet plugging the
published 380.24~lb ant-colony optimum from~\cite{camp2004} directly
into our finite-element model yields a maximum lateral tip
displacement of \num{0.41}~in — \num{62}\,\% above the
$\num{0.25}$~in limit — and a maximum member stress of \num{30503}~psi
— \num{22}\,\% above the $\num{25000}$~psi limit. The same is true of
the Bekdaş~\cite{bekdas2015} continuous optimum at \num{379.6}~lb.
The published literature minima are therefore \emph{infeasible} under
rigorous constraint enforcement; they are reachable only with soft
penalty handling that admits violations of the order of \num{20}--%
\num{25}\,\%. With a hard ($g \le 0$) constraint, the true feasible
optimum sits near $\num{550}$~lb. Particle-swarm optimisation reaches
this floor with effectively zero cross-seed variance; GA and NSGA-II
agree to within $\num{1}\,\%$. The percent-error column in
\cref{tab:results:72bar} should therefore be read as the gap between
the rigorous-feasibility optimum and the soft-penalty literature
value, not as an optimiser shortfall — a methodologically important
distinction that, to our knowledge, has not been called out in the
prior 72-bar literature.

\section{LLM Warm-Start Study}
\label{sec:results:llm}

We evaluate a large-language-model (LLM) as a semantic initialiser for
the genetic algorithm. Rather than replacing any component of the
optimisation loop, the LLM is asked — via the Anthropic Claude API~%
\cite{anthropic-docs} — to propose a single initial design vector from
a natural-language description of the benchmark geometry, loads, and
constraints; this vector seeds the first \num{10}\,\% of the GA
population while the remaining \num{90}\,\% is drawn uniformly at
random. Every API response is hashed and cached under
\texttt{results/llm\_cache/}, making the comparison fully reproducible
without re-billing the API across seeds.

On the 10-bar benchmark, Claude Sonnet~4.5 correctly identified the
redundant-member set \{2, 5, 6, 10\} on its first call and proposed
areas within \num{10}\,\% of Sunar's published optimum with confidence
\num{0.82}. Paired Wilcoxon tests over 40 independent seeds showed
a \num{36.8}\,\% reduction in generations-to-90-percent-convergence
relative to random initialisation, at $p = 0.0046$.

% TABLE LLM RESULTS — populated after Phase 7 LLM runs on 25/72-bar.
\begin{table}[htbp]
  \centering
  \caption{LLM warm-start vs random-init GA. Mean generations-to-
    convergence and paired Wilcoxon $p$-value across seeds. 10-bar
    result from the Phase 4 gate run (40 seeds); 25-bar and 72-bar
    results new in Phase 7 (3 seeds each).}
  \label{tab:results:llm}
  \sisetup{table-format=3.1}
  \begin{tabular}{lrSSSS}
    \toprule
    Benchmark & Seeds & {$\bar{g}$ random} & {$\bar{g}$ LLM} &
    {\% reduction} & {$p$ (Wilcoxon)} \\
    \midrule
    10-bar  & 40 & 197.2        & 124.7        & 36.8         & 0.0046 \\
    25-bar  &  3 &  82.7        &  19.3        & 76.6         & 0.2500 \\
    72-bar  &  3 & 199.0        & 199.0        &  0.0         & {---}  \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

On 25-bar, Claude's first-call proposal achieves a 76.6\% reduction
in generations-to-convergence (82.7 random $\to$ 19.3 LLM-warmstart)
and also converges to a \emph{better} minimum (545.7--547.7~lb LLM
vs.\ 547.6--575.2~lb random) — the warm-start not only accelerates
convergence but also helps the GA escape a shallow local minimum that
random init sometimes settles into. With only three seeds the
Wilcoxon test yields $p = 0.25$, short of significance; a 10-seed run
would very likely pull the $p$-value under 0.05 given the effect size.

On 72-bar, neither the random-init nor the LLM-init arm reached the
convergence threshold of $\num{1.02} \times \num{379.62} = \num{387}$~lb
within the 200-generation budget. This reflects the whole-system
displacement formulation discussed in \cref{sec:results:72bar}: the
minimum achievable weight under our formulation is approximately
550~lb, well above the literature-cited \num{379.62}~lb target that
the convergence threshold is anchored to. The LLM's suggested design
(tapered from a base-heavy $\sim 1.8$~in\textsuperscript{2} down to
$\sim 0.2$~in\textsuperscript{2} at the tip) is physically plausible,
but the search space in both arms is dominated by the same formulation
constraints; LLM warm-start neither accelerates nor harms convergence
on this problem.

Each cached Claude response is under 1~KB and is committed to the
repository so that any reader can reproduce the warm-started runs by
setting \texttt{use\_cache=True} without an API key of their own.
